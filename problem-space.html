The failure, in one picture

A found person and a searching family, in the same city, who never meet.

Center A registers a confused elderly man with no phone. Two kilometres away, Center B takes a frantic daughter's report. Both records exist. Neither system can read the other. The match that would reunite them in minutes instead takes hours — or never happens.

Today: each center is an island. Search stops at the door.

The one hard problem worth owning

Underneath every angle is the same task: entity resolution.

Matching sparse, multilingual, partial records of the same human across ten unconnected lists. Exact-match SQL breaks on every quirk above. Fuzzy reasoning over names, languages, places, age and description is exactly what an LLM does well — and it's defensible: you're not replacing the centers, you're the layer that makes their records mutually visible.

01
Collapse duplicates. New report arrives → is this someone already reported elsewhere? Kill the 8% noise before it wastes searcher time.
02
Match found ↔ missing. A person picked up at a center/hospital → rank the open reports they could be, across all centers.
03
Build on what exists. 1,280 cameras, 85 chokepoints, 14 stations, 32 zones are already mapped. Use the geography; don't rebuild it.
04
Degrade gracefully. Networks die on snan days. The layer must work offline and through a human operator for phoneless pilgrims.
